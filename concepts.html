Key Concepts

The budget

Structural deficit

A structural deficit happens when a government's regular expenses grow faster than its regular revenue. The gap doesn't go away on its own: it repeats every year. This is different from a one-time revenue shortfall, which reserves or short-term cuts can fix. The Government Finance Officers Association says a budget is "structurally balanced" only when regular revenue covers regular expenses, without using reserves or one-time money.

See it in: How We Got Here, What's Next

General Fund

The main operating fund a city uses to pay for day-to-day services, such as schools, police, fire, and public works. It's funded mainly by property taxes, state aid, and local receipts.

See it in: Budget Explorer, Capital Plan

Enterprise Fund

A separate fund for services meant to pay for themselves through user fees, not general tax revenue, such as water and sewer service. Debt and expenses in an enterprise fund are that fund's responsibility, not the General Fund's.

See it in: Capital Plan

Free cash

Unspent revenue from prior years, certified annually by the state. It's available for one-time uses, like capital projects or reserve deposits. It's not meant to fund ongoing operating costs.

See it in: Capital Plan, Budget Explorer

Stabilization fund

A reserve account, sometimes called a rainy day fund, set aside for future use, such as covering shortfalls or unexpected costs. Spending from it typically requires a two-thirds vote of the city council.

See it in: City Data

ACFR (Annual Comprehensive Financial Report)

The official yearly financial report a city prepares, audited by an independent accounting firm. It's the main source for verified historical budget and debt figures used across this site.

See it in: City Data, Sources

Financial Forecasting Committee (FFC)

A City of Beverly committee that produces multi-year revenue and expenditure forecasts and publishes minutes on the city's fiscal outlook. This site's structural deficit figures and expenditure growth assumptions come from its published forecasts and meeting minutes.

See it in: How We Got Here, What's Next, Sources

Prop 2½ and property taxes

Massachusetts Proposition 2½, enacted in 1980, limits how much a city or town's property tax levy can grow each year. The terms below build on each other, starting with the levy itself.

Levy

The property tax revenue a city or town raises through real and personal property taxes. It is the largest source of revenue for most Massachusetts cities and towns.

Levy ceiling

The maximum a community's levy limit can ever be: 2.5% of the total full and fair cash value of all taxable property in the community. The levy limit can never exceed the levy ceiling.

Levy limit

The maximum amount of property tax a community is allowed to raise in a given fiscal year. It's calculated by starting with the prior year's levy limit, then adding an automatic 2.5% increase, new growth, and any voter-approved overrides. The total is capped at the levy ceiling.

See it in: Peer Comparison

New growth

The increase a community is allowed to add to its levy limit each year, based on new construction, subdivisions, and other real additions to the tax base. It's calculated by multiplying the increase in assessed value from those changes by the prior year's tax rate. Growth from property revaluation doesn't count.

Override

A permanent increase to a community's levy limit, approved by majority vote. The override amount becomes part of the levy limit base and grows by 2.5% each year afterward, same as the rest of the limit. It cannot push the levy limit above the levy ceiling.

See it in: Override Calculator, What's Next

Debt exclusion

A temporary increase to a community's levy limit or ceiling, approved by voters, to pay debt service on a specific project. It lasts only for the life of the debt and never becomes part of the levy limit base, unlike an override. A related tool, a capital outlay expenditure exclusion, works the same way, but it funds a capital project directly for one year instead of paying off debt over time.

See it in: Capital Plan

Excess levy capacity

The gap between a community's levy limit and what it actually levies that year. It's a one-time opportunity: unused capacity from one year can't be carried forward or recaptured later. The following year's levy limit still increases on its own, regardless.

See it in: Peer Comparison

Source: "Levy Limits: A Primer on Proposition 2½," Massachusetts Department of Revenue, Division of Local Services (rev. 6/07).

Tax classification

Tax classification (the "CIP shift")

State law lets a city apply a higher tax rate to commercial, industrial, and personal property than to residential and open space property, up to a state-set maximum. This is sometimes called shifting the tax burden. Instead of taxing every property owner at the same rate, the city assigns a larger share of the total tax levy to commercial and industrial properties, and a smaller share to homeowners, within limits set by state law.
