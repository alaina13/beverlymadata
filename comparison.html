How Beverly Compares

Beverly's finances do not exist in isolation. Comparing tax rates, spending patterns, and available levy capacity against similarly sized North Shore communities helps clarify which pressures are unique to Beverly and which are shared. This page compares Beverly to Danvers, Salem, and Peabody using data reported to the Massachusetts Division of Local Services.

Levy capacity: how much room is left

Massachusetts caps how much a city's total property tax levy can grow each year: 2.5% automatically, plus the value of new construction, known as new growth, under Proposition 2½. A town's levy limit is the maximum it is legally allowed to raise in a given year. Excess levy capacity is the gap between that limit and what the town actually chose to raise. A town sitting close to its levy limit has little room to raise additional revenue without either an override vote or cutting spending elsewhere.

Tax base and rate structure

Massachusetts law lets cities and towns split their property tax rate into two tiers: one rate for residential and open space property, and a higher rate for commercial, industrial, and personal property (CIP). This is called tax classification, or a shift. Instead of taxing every property owner at the same rate, the city assigns a larger share of the total tax levy to the commercial and industrial base and a smaller share to homeowners, within limits set by state law.

The tables below show two related but different numbers: how large each town's commercial and industrial tax base actually is as a share of total assessed value, and how much higher that town has set its commercial rate relative to its residential rate. A town can only shift so much burden onto a base that size. Beverly has the smallest commercial and industrial tax base of the four communities, so even with its commercial rate set nearly as high relative to residential as Salem's or Peabody's, it produces less relief for residential taxpayers overall.
